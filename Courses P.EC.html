Philosophy & Ethics of Conservation

History

Conservation, a concept that has evolved over millennia, is much older than commonly believed. The philosophy and ethics of conservation’s origins go back to early human interactions with nature when we began recognizing the value of certain species and landscapes. As we are a part of nature, we need it to survive. Early, conservation was seen from both a spiritual context and the observations of hunter-gatherers when they saw how fire and hunting tools impacted our environment. Over time, these ideas evolved into more structured ethical frameworks, especially during the rise of science.


In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, thinkers such as Ralph Waldo Emerson, Aldo Leopold, and John Muir became figures in the development of conservation philosophy. Conservationists’ philosophy was split into two approaches: preservation versus conservation. Briefly, preservation is based on non-utilization values while conservation is based on sustainable resourcism.


Some examples: preservation originated with Ralph Waldo Emerson and Henry David Thoreau and was popularized by John Muir. Conservation as resource management was articulated by Gifford Pinchot.

Introduction to Conservation Ethics

Conservation ethics examine the delicate balance between human needs and the intrinsic value of nature. It addresses questions of:


1. Sustainability: maintaining ecological processes and preserving genetic diversity.
2. Intrinsic value and the rights of future generations.

Generally, conservation is the protection of wildlife from *irreversible harm*. However, as conservation has been practiced in various forms across different cultures and civilizations, it does not have one definition. It mainly depends on ethical arguments, aesthetic values, economic arguments, scientific values, and cultural differences. In this chapter, we will discuss ethics, economic arguments, and scientific value.

Ethics

Ethical arguments about conservation trace back to ancient times, when humans first revered nature and sacred species. However, the formal field of environmental ethics emerged more recently, with philosophers like E. Elliot shaping its foundation. Influential figures such as Ralph Waldo Emerson, Aldo Leopold, Henry David Thoreau, and John Muir were instrumental in establishing schools of thought centered on conservation. Each proposed unique perspectives: Leopold advocated for maintaining system complexity for sustainable use, others emphasized equity and efficiency by minimizing waste, while some viewed nature as an expression of divine creation, valued for its beauty, healing properties, and restorative power.


An ongoing debate exists about the intrinsic rights of species to exist, with conservationists arguing for their inherent value. However, skeptics note that ethics are human constructs, pointing out that biodiversity fluctuated naturally long before humans. This argument also intersects with issues of animal welfare and the balance between species' rights and human needs. While some argue humans are merely part of natural extinction processes, counterarguments highlight the dangers of allowing "natural" controls like famine, disease, or pollution to regulate human populations, as these would lead to widespread suffering.


The concept of "intergenerational equity" emphasizes the moral responsibility to preserve resources for future generations. This idea, rooted in Roosevelt’s stewardship ethic, reflects global commitments, such as the Convention on Biological Diversity, to conserve resources for present and future benefit. Yet, this view faces criticism from those who argue that immediate human needs outweigh uncertain long-term conservation benefits. Philosophical debates also grapple with species harmful to humans, like malaria-carrying mosquitoes, questioning whether their conservation is justified.


Irreversibility underpins conservation ethics—once species are extinct, most cannot be recreated, especially given the vast number yet to be discovered or understood. This irreplaceability strengthens the ethical argument for preventing extinction, even if it involves short-term sacrifices in human welfare. However, implementing precautionary principles often sparks tension, as economic costs or job losses may outweigh perceived risks. Conservationists advocate for cautious action in the face of uncertainty, arguing against "unplanned experiments" on ecosystems with irreversible consequences.


The "Deep Ecology" movement, a spiritual and philosophical stance, challenges anthropocentrism by emphasizing interconnectedness, minimal consumption, and respect for all life forms, including abiotic elements. Although this ideology often clashes with scientific approaches, its principles—rooted in the writings of thinkers like J. Muir and Wordsworth—carry significant weight in shaping policy and inspiring conservation ethics. Ultimately, the dialogue between ethics, practicality, and policy highlights the complexity of balancing humanity’s needs with the natural world’s preservation.

Economic

The economic arguments for conservation can be broadly divided into two categories: 'direct use value' and 'indirect use value'. Direct use involves harvesting resources from microbes, animals, and plants, while indirect use refers to the ecosystem services provided by species and habitats, such as rainfall promotion, flood prevention, pollination, and soil protection and aeration. These services play a crucial role in stabilizing the planetary environment, a concept explored in the Gaia theory.


Norman Myers was one of the pioneering scientists to discuss the importance of use values, with his works The Sinking Ark (1979) and The Primary Source (1984) raising awareness about the biodiversity crisis. E. S. Ayensu and others furthered the argument for the conservation of genetic resources, which refers to any biodiversity feature under genetic control that is economically valuable.


Direct use also includes harvesting parts of animals and plants, such as wool from llamas, as well as products like wood, straw, and dung, which are used for construction materials. Biodiversity also provides a range of oils, pharmaceuticals, and medical benefits. Most food comes from a small number of domesticated species, with only 20 species supplying 90% of the world’s food energy, half of which comes from wheat and rice.


When it comes to drugs, many are derived from natural sources, such as plant-based compounds that block enzymes or influence genes and neuroreceptors. Vaccines, often derived from disease organisms, may play a crucial role in treating diseases like AIDS or malaria. Historically, nearly all medicines in tribal societies came from natural products, and over 55% of the top 150 prescription drugs in the USA still contain compounds from nature. However, as the pharmaceutical industry turns to high-tech solutions, the role of biodiversity in providing new drugs, food, oils, and other products may decrease in the future.


The development of new drugs typically takes several years, and pharmaceutical companies often prefer modifying existing drugs because it’s quicker and more cost-effective. This lack of investment in biodiversity conservation is also driven by the 'discounting' issue, where future economic benefits are seen as less valuable than immediate ones. Similarly, many ecosystem services, such as hydrological stabilization, may be replaced with simpler systems or engineering solutions, although this may not fully replicate the functions of natural systems. If biodiversity is lost without being properly documented, we may permanently close off certain options, making a precautionary approach to conservation crucial.

Scientific values

From medicine to engineering, science has come from studying how organisms function and interact. Who would’ve thought that toxins from some of the world’s deadliest creatures would end up saving lives in the form of life-saving drugs? It's like nature’s version of an accidental superhero origin story! Meanwhile, delving into metabolic processes and animal behavior has helped shape everything from the quirky field of evolutionary psychology to predicting how fish, or even disease populations, might behave next.


And let’s not forget about biomimetics. Basically, the cool science of copying nature’s genius to solve human problems. Inspired by how a bird’s wing flaps just right, we’ve created everything from airplanes to bridges that stand the test of time. So, the next time someone tells you to “think outside the box,” remind them that sometimes, nature already has the answer.

Gaia

In 1967, James E. Lovelock introduced the concept of "Gaia," which stemmed from his observation that Earth maintains certain environmental conditions that are surprisingly stable, even though these states seem inherently improbable. For instance, Earth's atmosphere contains an unusually high amount of oxygen compared to Mars and Venus, where the atmospheres and surface rocks have reached chemical equilibrium, leaving little free oxygen. It is also remarkable that oxygen and methane coexist in Earth's atmosphere, as they would typically react with one another. Additionally, Earth's diurnal temperature variation is smaller than Mars due to the insulating effects of clouds, and the planet's surface is notably alkaline with abundant free water.


While Mars and Venus likely once had substantial water, much of it has been lost due to photodissociation—a process where sunlight breaks water molecules apart, releasing hydrogen, which escapes into space. On Earth, abundant atmospheric oxygen slows this loss, helping to retain water. Most of this oxygen is produced through photosynthesis in the oceans, and life within the oceans has reduced atmospheric carbon dioxide, which would otherwise amplify the greenhouse effect. Lovelock argued that Earth's abnormal physical and chemical characteristics result from the presence of life, with its biotic and abiotic components intricately connected.


Over time, discoveries have strengthened the Gaia concept. For example, W. D. Hamilton and T. M. Lenton proposed that marine microbes might play a significant role in forming clouds, dispersing themselves via tiny droplets. Microbes have even been found to be active within clouds. However, human activities like ozone depletion and overfishing are unpredictably affecting these systems, making the conservation of ocean ecosystems more critical.


The Gaia hypothesis was initially controversial, not due to the observed stability of Earth's systems but because biologists disagreed with Lovelock's suggestion that life evolved to stabilize the planet for its benefit, portraying Earth as a super-organism with a homeostatic mechanism. Lovelock's original "Daisyworld" model, which demonstrated this stability, was criticized for having an evolutionary bias, as pointed out by Hamilton. Yet, recent research has shifted the perspective, suggesting that evolutionary advantages for individual organisms might incidentally contribute to global stability.


Mathematical models, such as the "Dam World" model by Henderson and Hamilton, support this idea. In this model, a stream flows into a valley, and various species either build or erode a dam, affecting the water level. Systems with higher species diversity tend to stabilize the water level, implying that ecosystems with more species are inherently more stable due to density-dependent interactions like competition, predation, and disease. Complexity theory further supports the idea that complex systems with numerous interactions naturally gravitate toward stable states, known as "attractors." This research suggests that biodiversity loss and ecosystem simplification may lead to unpredictable and potentially irreversible planetary changes.


If Earth's stability—such as its oxygen levels, low greenhouse gas concentrations, abundant water, and moderate temperature—is indeed a result of biodiversity, preserving as many species as possible becomes essential. The Gaia hypothesis emphasizes the interconnectedness of species and advocates for a precautionary approach to conservation, highlighting biodiversity's crucial role in maintaining Earth's stability.
