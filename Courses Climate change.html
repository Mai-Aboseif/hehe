Climate change

*Introduction to Climate Change*

You may have heard of 'climate change' quite a lot in recent years. You may have been wondering what the fuss over climate change is all about and what exactly climate is changing into. Well, Earth's climate has changed over billions of years, and this change is mainly due to powerful forces from within the Earth and from the surroundings around it. Look at the molecule carbon dioxide. It has powerful properties of absorbing heat. There isn't a lot of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere, so any changes in carbon dioxide have the potential to change the atmosphere and the Earth in a big way. Over time, some of the carbon contained in carbon dioxide gas is locked away in the rocks of the Earth's lithosphere, thanks to what is often called the rock cycle. But with massive burns of fossil fuels going on during the last few hundred years, more carbon dioxide has been released in a few decades compared to what nature takes care of through the rock cycle in a few centuries. And with too much carbon dioxide staying in the air, Earth now holds onto more heat than it allows to go back into space.


So, carbon dioxide makes Earth a more comfortable place to live. But too much of a good thing may not be so good, as the extra heat could cause the polar ice to melt, storms to become more violent and shift where they occur, and heavy rainfall, droughts, and even forest fires to become more common. While the contours of how exactly the planet will change due to human activities are still being worked out, a number of changes have become apparent. They are all related to human activities that are changing the atmosphere, oceans, and land surfaces of the Earth. That's why we know climate change is real and relevant to humans. While climate has always changed, the pace of change has not been this quick in all the periods that life has existed on our lovely planet. Indeed, the global mean temperature has increased to such an extent in the recent period. With the rapidly changing climate, the ability of species to adapt to the new environment is stretched while they try to survive and maintain life situations on the ever-changing planet.

*Definition and Causes*

Although climate change and global warming are used interchangeably, they are slightly different. Global warming is a part of climate change and refers to the increase in temperature close to the surface of the Earth. Climate change refers to changes in the planet’s average temperature or changes in regional climate characteristics over time scales. What are some of the key factors behind climate change? Emerging scientific data lends further support that the recent surge in global warming is caused by human activity. Advances in technology, increased population, and an explosion in industrialization have increased the human population’s energy demand and have rapidly consumed huge amounts of fuel in our ecosystem. Also, human activities in the twentieth century have played a main part in increasing Earth’s greenhouse effect. Burning fossil fuels for energy introduced large quantities of heat-trapping gases into Earth’s atmosphere. These gases prevent heat from leaving the atmosphere, which leads to an increase of heat trapped within the atmosphere. Over time, this heat rise can affect the planet’s weather, ocean currents, and climate.

*Historical Context*

Let's fast forward again, this time to the 20th century. Throughout the 20th century, carbon dioxide and other gases have been released into the atmosphere. The amount of carbon dioxide has increased by about 25%, and the concentration of the others has also grown. Part of the reason is deforestation to make way for farmland, ranching, and development, but most of the additions come from burning coal, oil, gasoline, or gas to produce more energy for our homes, cars, factories, businesses, and you name it. The atmosphere now contains almost 1.5 trillion metric tons of carbon, more than 3.5 trillion pounds, far more than any time within the last 20 million years. How much is 1.5 trillion metric tons? It's such a huge amount that an annual increase of only 6 million metric tons still adds up to a substantial fraction of the total each year, producing a big annual change in the atmosphere’s carbon dioxide content.

*Impact on the Environment*

One of the greatest impacts of the burning of fossil fuels is on the environment. With more carbon in the atmosphere, plants and trees can grow faster. They absorb more carbon, but the forests are shrinking because of human activities. The roots of trees hold the topsoil of Earth, and a lot of fertile soil is washed away with water after the trees are cut. Trees also give us the oxygen we breathe, slow down the energy of the sun, and keep Earth's temperature low. Without trees, the Earth would heat, and temperatures could rise even faster. But when we burn them, greenhouse gases are released into the atmosphere, and the trees are gone.


Deforestation must stop soon if high temperatures are to be prevented in the future. A big change in the way trees are used for paper and construction is needed. Today, too many greenhouse gases come from the fact that solid waste is burned in landfills and power plants. This has to be done as little as possible if these gases are to be transformed into renewable energy or buried underground. Coal is the dirtiest fossil fuel because so many impurities are released when it is burned. Using oil and natural gas is better. They burn more efficiently and have fewer impurities. But still, they also need to go. Our sun is a great source of energy. A lot of energy is in its light, and this heat is stored in the ground and air. Solar energy could be used more. But for the time being, it is a little difficult to do, and some big and old equipment will be needed to work on a day that looks special. The idea is to convert sunlight into electricity.
