Introduction to Biodiversity

Welcome to the course on biodiversity, where we will explore the incredible variety of life on Earth, its importance to human well-being, and the challenges we face in conserving it. Biodiversity, a contraction of "biological diversity," encompasses the variety of living organisms, ecosystems, and ecological processes that sustain life on our planet. It forms the foundation of human development, supports environmental stability, and charges economic and cultural health across societies.

Biodiversity Definition

According to the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD), biodiversity is the variety of life on Earth. “The variability among living organisms from all sources, including, amongst others, terrestrial, marine, and other aquatic ecosystems and the ecological complexes of which they are a part; this includes diversity within species, between species, and of ecosystems,” CBD stated.


Biodiversity on Earth results from 3.5 billion years of evolution. Although the fascinating biological diversity of our planet, it’s exponentially degrading. Our unsustainable practices have caused extinctions for over 45,000 years and it’s ever-growing! Biodiversity management is one of the most urgent challenges facing environmental conservation as we must address the problem soon or species extinction will be faster than us.

Why Biodiversity Matters

Throughout Earth's history, biodiversity has undergone cycles of expansion and extinction. However, current extinction rates, driven by human activity, are higher than historical background rates. Scientists now anticipate “the sixth mass extinction.”


When habitats are reduced by 90%, they often lose about half of their species. Using real-world examples like the Atlantic Forests of Brazil and the Amazon rainforest, we will analyze how biodiversity loss affects not just species but entire ecosystems and the services they provide to humans, such as clean air, water, and fertile soil. This emphasizes the importance of biodiversity not only for our Earth’s balance but also for human well-being by providing essential ecosystem services such as:


- *Provisioning services*: Food, water, timber, and medicine.
- *Regulating services*: Climate regulation, pollination, and disease control.
- *Cultural services*: Inspiration, recreation, and spiritual enrichment.
- *Supporting services*: Nutrient cycling and soil formation.


Imagine a world without bees. These tiny pollinators, a small yet vital cog in the biodiversity machine, are crucial for growing crops that feed billions.

Biodiversity Threats

As the natural environment erodes, species extinction rates have soared to 100–1,000 times the prehistoric background levels, with predictions of accelerating losses in the coming decades. While larger species like vertebrates and plants are often studied, less is known about the extinction rates of invertebrates and microbes, which likely dominate biodiversity losses. For instance, Britain’s comprehensive Red Data Book shows the invertebrate extinction rates of 1–5% per century. When data is scaled globally, it translates into the loss of thousands of species annually.


In the following section, we will study how and why these extinctions occur.

Extinction

Extinction is the loss of prominent species like the passenger pigeon, dodo, etc. The majority of extinctions likely occur among less-studied, species-rich groups such as invertebrates and microbes. Since many species don't leave fossils, their extinction often goes unnoticed. Without knowing the total number of species, it's impossible to determine the absolute number of extinctions occurring annually. However, we can make rough estimates about the proportion of species lost in a particular area or group based on ecological patterns.


One critical pattern is the species-area relationship, which shows that smaller habitats tend to support fewer species. For instance, an oceanic island one-tenth the size of another may host roughly half the number of species. This relationship applies to isolated environments like mountains or lakes and helps predict how habitat destruction impacts biodiversity. If a habitat is reduced by 90%, such as through deforestation or drainage, it might eventually lose half its species. However, questions remain about how habitat fragmentation mirrors island isolation and how long it takes for species numbers to stabilize (a process called “relaxation”).


Studies of fragmented ecosystems, like the Atlantic Forests of Brazil (of which less than 10% remains) initially suggested fewer extinctions than predicted. However, researchers such as Brooks and Balmford have identified species in these areas as “living dead,” species unlikely to survive in the long term. This concept of “extinction debt” reflects species already doomed due to habitat loss but not yet extinct, meaning biodiversity may continue declining over decades.


Using the species-area relationship, we can estimate species loss based on habitat reduction. For example, losing 90% of the Amazon rainforest could result in the extinction of half its species, potentially representing a significant portion of Earth's biodiversity. If baseline species richness data is available, we can even estimate the number of species likely to be lost within specific groups, such as birds or plants. However, predicting the timescale for these losses is much harder. Continental fragments may have lower extinction rates than islands due to more generalist species but could also face higher rates from “edge effects” caused by surrounding disturbed habitats.


Natural extinction has always been part of Earth's history, with 95–99% of all species that ever existed now extinct. The fossil record reveals periods of mass extinction, such as the Permian-Triassic event, which wiped out 95% of marine species, and the Cretaceous event, marking the end of the dinosaurs. Between these mass events, background extinction rates suggest an average vertebrate species lifetime of 1–2 million years. However, over the past century, extinction rates for large vertebrates have accelerated drastically, with species lifetimes dropping to 10,000 years—and potentially as low as 200–400 years in the future. This increase represents extinction rates 100–1,000 times higher than the prehistoric background level and possibly 10,000 times higher in this century. This rapid loss is now recognized as the sixth mass extinction, with humanity as a driving force.


Britain, with a rich history of species documentation, offers rare insights into invertebrate extinction. Using Red Data Book records, the estimated extinction rate for British invertebrates is 1–5% per century, aligning with global patterns for other groups. Applying this rate to the conservative estimate of 5–15 million species suggests that 50,000–750,000 species could be lost per century—equivalent to 500–7,500 species annually or 1–20 species per day. While thought-provoking, these estimates are uncertain due to variations across taxa and ecosystems, as well as the unknown total number of species. It’s safer to acknowledge the limits of our knowledge and prioritize biodiversity conservation.


Biodiversity embraces various living organisms, the ecosystems they are part of, and the ecological processes that interconnect all the elements. It is the natural foundation of human development and a key component of individuals’ and societies' environmental, economic, social, and cultural health. The value of biodiversity, a wealth of life and biological resources, is mostly irreplaceable. Biodiversity is our conserved biological capital. Over the centuries, human use of this capital has increased. At a local level and a regional scale, much of our surviving stock of biodiversity has been eroded and degraded. The loss has been relatively slow and insidious, escaping the notice of many for whom natural diversity has become less and less a part of their everyday life experience. Today, at the international level, the picture is more obviously one of global change that is irreversible. Trends in our key biological assets that only very recently were regarded as virginal are now consistently alarming and predictable. The habitats that support threatened biodiversity have eroded at unprecedented rates. Species are becoming extinct through a combination of habitat loss, intensification of traditional land use, and pollution. These processes are shifting patterns of global biodiversity at all scales. The diversity of genes within species is also threatened. United efforts at the international, regional, national, and local levels are essential to meet the conservation challenges posed by the erosion of biodiversity. The income from natural resources that is currently fueling development in many countries is provided largely by oil, natural gas, coal, and minerals. Predictably, the value of the natural resources now underpinning global economies will fluctuate according to changing economic and political demands. This cannot be so for the stock of the planet's biodiversity.
