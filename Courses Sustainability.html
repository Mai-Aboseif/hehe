Introduction to Sustainability

Living sustainably is an essential part of what it means to be human. The complex set of problems related to the ways we live portrays a picture of a world tearing up the basic fabric of life. From acid rain to the alarming decline in the supply of clean air and fresh water, from an operating loss in the earth's forests to the loss of hundreds of animal and plant species each year, from ever-faster depletion of the world's fisheries to the frightening possibilities of massive climatic changes, the world seems to be on the brink of a potential disaster. From the ozone layer to the earth's capacity to soak up our wastes, we are overloading the world's ability to sustain the current quality of our health and lives.


The challenge is to take the long-term view and step up to the moral and community responsibility of creating a generation born satiated with all human needs. This does not simply mean a world that survives, but one capable of sustaining the healthy, happy, creative lives of our children, and their children, and their children's children, centuries hence. Indeed, the challenge of living sustainably is also an exciting opportunity. Practically every aspect of human existence with which we are familiar now must be reimagined and redesigned within a sustainability framework that includes both the built and the natural environments. The opportunities are enormous, but the challenges are equally large and numerous. We are in the midst of a great transition period, from a world built on human-dominated natural systems to one yet to be created – a world that sustains both the natural environment of the earth and those who live on it.

Historical Development of Sustainability

Sustainability is not a new concept. The concept of sustainability has developed from the case of local environmental degradation and institutional failure, which have existed for centuries. Studies of forest usage, drought-affected areas, and soil degradation all infer that the problem is local to the people who live on the land. The expression of 'sustainable development' finally attracted global attention during the 1980s, from which it was widespread through academic and political networks. However, the key consideration during this period was the work of an independent organization established to consider the world's environmental concerns.


The term sustainable development was defined in 1987 as 'to develop a secure and just world that can be sustained indefinitely.' In essence, sustainable development considers future generations, in that their rights to reasonable development will be met at the same level as the present ones. It is thus a fundamental principle of intergenerational equity. Initially, the phrase attracted a great deal of criticism due to the unclear qualifications on what 'secure' and 'reasonable development' really were, with sustainable development seen by applicants as a motherhood statement; everyone could agree that good things were worth it. Additionally, the process of taking cross-section level selection was difficult. Official governmental interpretations were varied and often quite diversionary from the original aims of the phrase, although the main objective was agreed upon as being an aspiration for all consumption and extraction to be within the carrying capacity of the earth's environment in perpetuity.

Definition of Sustainability

Sustainability is about the capacity to endure. For humans, sustainability is the long-term maintenance of well-being, which has environmental, social, and economic dimensions. Sustainability has also been described as a science, which looks at the world as a system. It requires interdisciplinarity because each field has its own specialization, but the overall task is to understand the interactions that bind human society to the surroundings, its natural, and manmade environments. Sustainability can be differential, philosophically termed as weak versus strong sustainability. The aim of weak form sustainability is for the primary use of the resource to be justified by the ensuing loss of value in the nonrenewable form of capital, which can be invested in other forms of capital, whereas a strong form of sustainability aspires to preserve the stock of a nonrenewable resource, substituting, if possible, with a renewable resource, all forms of capital.


One of the main long-term plans is to restore the use of natural resources. If we were to follow the path our current society is following, the impact of this approach could not achieve the restoration target. A part of this situation occurs not because of the interest in using renewable resources, but because of the rapidly growing human population that is affecting Earth's resources. By 2050, global natural resource consumption could triple, which corresponds to an entire planet. It is important to enable the natural capital system to produce a flow of key ecological goods and services: freshwater, food, timber, fiber, genetic resources, natural medicines, recycling and transformation of waste, soil fertility and protection, erosion control, crop pollination, and protection of biodiversity. At the same time, the natural capital system regulates ecological processes: the climate by carbon sequestration, greenhouse gas balance, the water cycle, the biogeochemical cycle, and the eutrophication process, pollutants, maintaining soil fertility and structure, pollination, natural pest control, and protection from zoonoses. Ultimately, it must ensure various ecological stages, ecosystem dynamics, and life are the source of a sense of wonder, delight, and spiritual experience.

*Why Is Sustainability Important?*

[The National Environmental Policy Act of 1969](https://www.epa.gov/nepa) (https://www.epa.gov/nepa) committed the United States to sustainability, declaring it a national policy “to create and maintain conditions under which humans and nature can exist in productive harmony, that permit fulfilling the social, economic and other requirements of present and future generations.”


In the years since NEPA was enacted, the public’s interest in sustainability has broadened. According to the National Research Council, there are many additional drivers for sustainability. In the areas where the US has seen considerable progress in sustainability, a common driver for sustainability efforts is citizens’ and other stakeholders’ concerns. In addition, sustainability practitioners are becoming more ambitious in their sustainability efforts and are working together to share best practices to ensure the greatest environmental, economic, and social impact.

Maximum Sustainable Yields

Maximum Sustainable Yields (MSYs) represent the highest harvesting rate that a population can sustain over time, where growth and reproduction compensate for the organisms or parts removed. The concept is based on *logistic growth* and *density-dependent competition. At low population densities, organisms reproduce at their maximum rate in resource-rich environments, resulting in exponential growth. However, as population density increases and resources become limited, competition reduces survival and reproduction rates. Eventually, the population stabilizes at a level called the carrying capacity, or **K*, though in reality, populations might crash or show more complex dynamics.


The logistic growth curve is modeled by the equation:
*dN/dt = r N((K − N)/K)*
Here, *N* is the population size, *r* is the maximum reproductive rate, and *K* is the carrying capacity. Adding harvesting to this equation gives:
*dN/dt = r N((K − N)/K) − H*
where *H* represents the harvesting rate. The idea is that population growth is slow when numbers are either very low (due to limited breeding pairs) or very high (due to intense competition). The highest growth rate—and thus the greatest yield—occurs at intermediate population densities. To achieve MSY, *N* should be kept below *K, with harvesting rates below the species’ maximum reproductive capacity (r*). Overharvesting, however, risks population collapse unless rates are quickly reduced.
A major challenge for MSY models is measuring *r, **K, and **N* accurately. While it might be possible to estimate the reproductive potential of a species (e.g., the number of eggs a fish lays), counting entire populations, such as cod or hardwood trees, is often extremely difficult. Moreover, *K* varies with environmental factors like climate and resource availability, making population management tricky. For instance, sablefish stocks in Alaska fluctuate with changing climate conditions, requiring cautious harvesting strategies to avoid overexploitation in low-productivity years.


When direct population data are unavailable, monitoring the effort needed to harvest a given yield can help indicate sustainability. For example, if it takes more time or energy to catch fish or find trees, this suggests overharvesting. Harvest levels should then be adjusted to allow populations to recover. This trial-and-error approach, called *adaptive management*, is a safer method in such cases.


MSYs are particularly challenging for species with low reproductive rates (like whales and elephants), as these populations recover slowly and are more vulnerable to overexploitation. *K-selected species* and social animals also face unique risks, with phenomena like the *Allee effect* worsening their vulnerability. For erratic populations, *proportional threshold harvesting*—where no harvest is allowed below a certain population size—has been suggested as a safer alternative, though it still requires regular monitoring.


In some cases, overharvesting leads to changes that are hard or impossible to reverse. For example, the collapse of Newfoundland cod stocks resulted in ecological shifts that have prevented recovery even a decade after fishing was banned. This highlights the delicate balance needed to ensure long-term sustainability in harvesting. Despite these challenges, examples like New Zealand’s fisheries quota system and New England’s protection of large lobsters show that with careful monitoring, policy enforcement, and ecological understanding, sustainable harvesting is possible.
