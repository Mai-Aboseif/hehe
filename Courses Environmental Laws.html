Environmental Laws

International laws

International laws can either be binding, meaning they are integrated into national legal systems, or act as commitments without direct legal enforcement. In the latter case, countries in breach of the agreement may receive assistance to fulfill their obligations, though they risk reputational damage or rejection by the global community. Most international environmental laws, often referred to as “legal instruments,” lack enforceable penalties like fines or sanctions. Instead, they typically require states to exercise *due diligence*—aiming to minimize environmental impacts rather than entirely prevent issues. Among the roughly 1,000 international laws relevant to conservation, some stand out for their influence.


One of the most significant is the *Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD), adopted during the 1992 Earth Summit. Its core objectives are the conservation of biodiversity, the sustainable use of its components, and the fair sharing of benefits arising from genetic resources. Signatory states acknowledge the inherent value of biodiversity—not just ecologically, but also economically, scientifically, and culturally—and recognize its conservation as a shared responsibility of humankind. The convention emphasizes **in situ conservation*, focusing on ecosystems and species in their natural environments, and advocates for an ecosystem-based, adaptive approach to management. While these commitments are powerful and could profoundly impact conservation efforts, not all nations have ratified or implemented the treaty effectively.


Another critical instrument is the *Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species (CITES)*, signed by over 150 countries. CITES regulates wildlife trade through three Appendices. Appendix I includes species prohibited from international trade. Appendix II lists species that require export permits to ensure trade monitoring, while Appendix III provides additional safeguards requested by specific countries. Decisions about species listings can be contentious, as seen in debates over the trade of elephant ivory. Enforcement largely relies on customs officials in exporting and importing countries, though identifying species often poses challenges. Broad classifications, such as listing entire groups like the cactus family, help mitigate this issue. Despite enforcement efforts, such as the seizure of over a million wildlife products in Britain between 1997 and 2002, much illegal trade remains undetected. Globally, wildlife trafficking is the third-largest illegal trade.

National and Regional Laws

At the national level, conservation laws are theoretically easier to enforce, as penalties like fines and imprisonment can be tailored to specific offenses. National laws also regulate activities that protect biodiversity, such as quarantine measures to prevent invasive species or anti-pollution laws safeguarding air and water quality. Additionally, legislation supports the establishment and management of protected areas like National Parks and Nature Reserves.


Conservation laws often focus on either species or habitats. For instance, in the United States, the *Endangered Species Act (ESA)* and the *National Forest Management Act* prioritize species. The ESA mandates the recovery of listed species to non-threatened levels and includes habitat protection measures. It also prohibits the importation of products from non-native listed species, such as caviar from beluga sturgeon. Enforcement extends to legal challenges by organizations like Riverkeeper, which hold developers and the government accountable for violations. In the UK, species-focused laws protect animals such as badgers or groups like birds, bats, and reptiles, while the *Wildlife and Countryside Act* provides additional species protections.

Locally, regulations may restrict certain activities, such as vehicle use, fire lighting, or dog-walking. Quota systems may also be introduced for harvesting resources, such as the licensing system for mushroom collection in Epping Forest, England. These localized measures complement national and international frameworks, creating multi-layered conservation strategies.
