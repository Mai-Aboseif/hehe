Ecosystem

Introduction to Ecosystems

One of the reasons the global ecosphere is both amazing and complex is that Earth sustains a large number and wide variety of ecosystems. An ecosystem is defined as a biological community plus all the abiotic factors in the area, meaning all living organisms in an area along with their physical and chemical environment. Ecosystems can range many orders in size, from microscopic to nearly global. Some examples of ecosystems of a particular size might include that of a massive redwood tree, a lake in a national park, and the tropical rainforest in a basin.


Quantum units of ecosystems are, of course, the millions of different species of green plants. Since their green pigments absorb the sun's energy, they are called producers, and they are at the bottom of the food chain. They are the only type of organism capable of using the sun's energy to make energy-rich organic molecules. We call these complex, energy-rich molecules the Earth's chemical currency. All of the other organisms in the ecosphere, from a tiny bacterium to a huge whale, must use producers, as well as other organisms, to obtain chemical food. The microbial food chain consists of a producer, followed by a primary consumer, a secondary consumer, and so on, to the top carnivore. As we go up the food chain, the number of individuals in a species decreases.

Ecosystem Definition

Ecosystem science is the study of interrelationships among organisms and between those organisms and their physical and social environments. There are a number of important words in this definition that require exploration to gain a full understanding of what ecosystem science is all about. Ecosystems are not the same as natural landscapes. For most people, ecosystems are a much broader idea than natural landscapes as they combine the natural environment with the people and built environments that are part of it. Components of all ecosystems can be seen from space and include abiotic environments, processes, and organisms.


Ecosystem science seeks to understand how and why the physical and social environments of Earth are changing, and the consequences of these changes on the economies and quality of life of nations and people, the environment, and the capacity of the Earth to support humanity. The first thing to notice is that ecosystems are all about relationships. One definition of an ecosystem is that it is a combination of the organisms within a given geographic area, the physical environment in which these organisms live, interact, and function, and the relationships among and between these organisms and the various aspects of their physical and social environments.

Types of Ecosystems

Ecosystems come in many shapes and sizes; they can be just a few square meters in area or spread over thousands of square kilometers. They can be found on land or in the sea, and they can exist in countries on all continents. Ecosystems can be as small as a bush or as large as a desert. Some ecosystems are simple, while others are very complex. Across the world, ecosystems share several common features while also being enormously variable because of differing local conditions. Because of this variability, ecosystems are studied at a variety of scales, from very large to small scales, such as a pond, tree, or even a rock, which are frequently used by students and educators who may not have access to the larger study sites or which can easily be manipulated in a laboratory.


The basic concept is that all living organisms within an ecosystem are dependent on and interact with each other. In the terrestrial environment, weather, temperature, and amount of rainfall will affect the types of plants that can grow. The types of plants present will affect the types of animals that can live in an ecosystem. The types of soil will affect the distribution of plants by making some plants grow better than others.
